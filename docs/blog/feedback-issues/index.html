1. Introduction#

The AI community extensively uses human feedback for training and evaluation. In the context of large language models (LLMs), this feedback is often collected as (pairwise) ranking feedback: given a prompt and two (or more) responses, annotators rank responses according to their preference. Such data is then directly used to train models (e.g. via RLHF (Kaufmann et al., 2023 Kaufmann, T., Weng, P., Bengs, V. & H√ºllermeier, E. (2023). A Survey of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2312.14925 )), or to evaluate models (e.g. in Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024 Chiang, W., Zheng, L., Sheng, Y., Angelopoulos, A., Li, T., Li, D., Zhang, H., Zhu, B., Jordan, M., Gonzalez, J. & Stoica, I. (2024). Chatbot Arena: An Open Platform for Evaluating LLMs by Human Preference. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2403.04132 )). As with any data, there are some common issues with ranking feedback that can negatively affect downstream use-cases — potentially resulting in misleading evaluation results or trained models that are poorly aligned to human values. Whilst issues with ranking feedback are frequently discussed separately, I have not come across a good overview of common issues.

This blog post aims to fill this gap: a list of the (in my opinion) most common issues with ranking feedback. This list is shaped by my own experience generating and working with ranking feedback datasets — and often running into some of these very same issues. I hope it helps!

2. Background: ranking feedback data#

In this blog post, I focus on feedback data in the context of LLM training and evaluation. In particular, I focus on ranking feedback – possibly the most common form of feedback used in the LLM context. In ranking feedback, each datapoint typically consists of the following parts: (A) a prompt, (B) multiple responses and a (C) general ranking annotation that orders the responses according to annotator preference. Often only two responses are ranked (Chiang et al., 2024 Chiang, W., Zheng, L., Sheng, Y., Angelopoulos, A., Li, T., Li, D., Zhang, H., Zhu, B., Jordan, M., Gonzalez, J. & Stoica, I. (2024). Chatbot Arena: An Open Platform for Evaluating LLMs by Human Preference. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2403.04132 ). Such feedback is also commonly referred to as pairwise preferences/feedback. Human-annotated ranking feedback is often simply referred to as human preferences or human feedback. In many datasets additional information beyond prompt, responses and ranking is included: for example, (D) additional annotations (e.g. regarding the truthfulness of each response) and (E) metadata about the models used to sample responses and annotator details (e.g. demographics or, if LLM-based, configuration parameters). The (pseudo-)yaml below illustrates this typical structure of a ranking feedback datapoint

3. Common issues#

As with any form of dataset, ranking feedback datasets frequently have issues affecting downstream uses. I split common issues into two separate lists: first issues related to the selection of prompts and responses (dataset design issues) and then issues related to the annotation of these datasets (annotation issues). Most issues affect both evaluation and training use-cases. Issues that are only relevant for one use-case are marked as such (e.g. ’eval only’). Below is an overview of the issues discussed.

Important note: Many issues can be unavoidable. Thus, whilst it is important to be aware that certain datasets have these issues, I do not think these issues reflect negatively on the dataset’s creators or their contribution. Collecting human data is expensive and messy. Large ranking feedback datasets are rarely made public, so any dataset contributions are very valuable even if they have issues.

For many issues below I add references. These references contain some form of discussion of the respective issue, sometimes (but not always) because the issues is observed in a newly introduced dataset.

4. Conclusion#

Ranking feedback can be flawed in many ways. Yet, the above list is undoubtedly incomplete: there are many dataset properties that are difficult to measure and haven’t been detected yet. Ranking feedback also has more general limitations: for example, relative rankings omit potentially important information about how much to responses differ. In general, given the enormous cost of both data collection and model training, really trying to understand ranking feedback datasets you use as well as possible seems well-justified – there are many potential issues to detect and mitigate. Exploring this open problem of issue detection, our recent work on Inverse Constitutional AI (Findeis et al., 2024 Findeis, A., Kaufmann, T., H√ºllermeier, E., Albanie, S. & Mullins, R. (2024). Inverse Constitutional AI: Compressing Preferences into Principles. Retrieved from http://arxiv.org/abs/2406.06560 ) aims to automatically find new kinds of annotator biases — beyond those already well-known. I think there is great potential to develop further (potentially similar) methods that can effectively discover and measure ranking feedback issues.
